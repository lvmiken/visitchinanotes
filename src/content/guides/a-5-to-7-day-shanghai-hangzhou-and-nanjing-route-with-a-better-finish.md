This route is for travelers who want East China to become richer, not just prettier.

Shanghai gives you polish and scale. Hangzhou gives you scenic release. Nanjing gives you a more grounded, historically serious finish. That combination often works better than Shanghai plus two gentler cities because the route keeps changing register.

## Why this sequence works

`Shanghai -> Hangzhou -> Nanjing` usually feels best because:

- Shanghai is the easiest opening city
- Hangzhou provides the cleanest middle reset
- Nanjing is a stronger closing city than Hangzhou because it can hold both an evening and a more meaningful final day

Emotionally, the route should feel like this:

- `Shanghai` opens
- `Hangzhou` loosens the shoulders
- `Nanjing` leaves the stronger final impression

That is why this route often suits travelers who want more range than `Shanghai + Hangzhou` but less fragmentation than the full four-city version.

## The 5-day version: selective, but real

This version works only if you resist overbuilding every stop.

### Days 1 to 2: Shanghai

Keep Shanghai strong but not overlong:

- one skyline-and-center layer
- one neighborhood-and-food layer

If Shanghai still feels incomplete after two days, the route may be trying to outrun its own anchor.

### Day 3: Hangzhou

Use Hangzhou to reset:

- [West Lake](/places/west-lake/)
- one supporting branch such as [Lingyin Temple](/places/lingyin-temple/) or [Longjing Village](/places/longjing-village/)

### Days 4 to 5: Nanjing

Arrive early enough that Nanjing gets:

- a real evening around [Confucius Temple and the Qinhuai River](/places/confucius-temple-and-qinhuai-river/)
- one proper historical day around [Presidential Palace](/places/presidential-palace/), [Nanjing City Wall](/places/nanjing-city-wall/), or [Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum](/places/sun-yat-sen-mausoleum/)

This is the shortest version that still gives the route a meaningful finish.

## The 6-day version: usually the best balance

For many first-time visitors, this is the strongest form of the route.

It gives you:

- `2.5 to 3 days` in Shanghai
- `1 night` in Hangzhou
- `2 days / 1 night` in Nanjing

That works because:

- Shanghai still feels whole
- Hangzhou actually changes the pace
- Nanjing gets enough room to feel like a city, not an obligation

## The 7-day version: when the close really matters

At seven days, the route gains emotional shape.

A strong version is:

- `Days 1 to 3`: Shanghai
- `Days 4 to 5`: Hangzhou
- `Days 6 to 7`: Nanjing

This works well if:

- Hangzhou deserves more than one compressed scenic loop
- Nanjing deserves more than one rushed daytime history block
- the route wants contrast, not only efficiency

## Why this route finishes better than a softer three-city loop

Some travelers know they do not want three gentle cities in a row.

That is where `Shanghai + Hangzhou + Nanjing` wins:

- Shanghai gives polish
- Hangzhou gives softness
- Nanjing gives consequence

The route ends on something more substantial than scenic afterglow.

## If you need to cut one city

Cut `Hangzhou` first if:

- scenery is not the missing layer
- you want a tighter urban-and-history route

Cut `Nanjing` first if:

- the trip wants to stay lighter
- history is not central to why you are adding a second or third East-China stop
