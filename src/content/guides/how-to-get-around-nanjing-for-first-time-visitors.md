Nanjing transport usually is easier than the city first feels on a planning map.

The mistake is rarely a lack of options.

The mistake is asking one transport mode to solve very different kinds of time:

- a station arrival
- a heavy historical block
- a Purple Mountain half day
- an old-city evening
- a tired return after dinner

Nanjing works better when you stop treating those as the same problem.

## The short answer

For many first-time visitors, the strongest Nanjing transport pattern is:

1. use `metro` for the big city moves
2. use `walking` for the best old-city and evening blocks
3. use `taxi or Didi` for luggage, late returns, bad weather, or awkward final legs
4. let the `hotel base` do part of the work before the day even begins

That usually is enough.

## Why Nanjing transport feels better when the route is edited

Nanjing is not a city that rewards constant bouncing.

It gets stronger when each half day has one clear role, for example:

- one museum or memorial block
- one Purple Mountain or city-wall block
- one old-city evening block

Once the day is shaped that way, the transport becomes much more forgiving.

Once it is not, the city starts feeling farther apart than it really is.

## When metro is usually the best choice

Metro is usually strongest when:

- you are moving between central districts
- the day is still in its broad movement phase
- weather is normal
- you are not carrying luggage
- the route is direct enough that a car adds little value

Metro often works especially well for:

- station arrival or departure days
- moving between a practical hotel base and a major history area
- short stays where efficiency matters more than scenic drift

## When walking should take over

This is the transport rule many first-time visitors miss.

Do not try to solve Nanjing entirely as a metro diagram.

Walking matters most once you already are in the right district, especially around:

- [Confucius Temple and the Qinhuai River](/places/confucius-temple-and-qinhuai-river/)
- [Laomendong](/places/laomendong/)
- some city-wall and old-core areas
- calmer blocks such as [Xuanwu Lake](/places/xuanwu-lake/)

Nanjing often feels best when metro gets you near the right zone, and walking does the actual city-reading.

## When taxi or Didi becomes smarter

Paying more often becomes worth it when the edges of the day start becoming the real problem.

That usually happens when:

- you have luggage
- the return is late
- the weather is poor
- the route already used a lot of walking
- one final connection would otherwise be clumsy enough to drag down the whole day

This is especially true after days that include:

- [Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum](/places/sun-yat-sen-mausoleum/)
- [Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum](/places/ming-xiaoling-mausoleum/)
- [Nanjing City Wall](/places/nanjing-city-wall/)

The smartest transport choice is often the one that protects your energy at the end, not the one that looked cheapest at the beginning.

## Buses exist, but they should not be your first headache

For many foreign first-time visitors, the practical transport hierarchy in Nanjing is simple:

- metro first
- Didi or taxi for awkward edges
- bus only when one exact route clearly improves the day

That does not mean buses are bad.

It means most short Nanjing stays do not need bus mastery in order to function well.

## Hotel area is the real transport decision most travelers make without noticing

Many Nanjing transport problems that look like mode problems are actually base problems.

The hotel choice quietly decides:

- whether the first evening works
- whether Qinhuai feels easy or annoying
- whether a short stay wastes time on repeated cross-city movement
- whether your last morning still behaves like part of the trip

## What this looks like on real Nanjing days

### Museum-led day

For a day built around [Presidential Palace](/places/presidential-palace/) or [Nanjing Museum](/places/nanjing-museum/), metro often handles the main movement cleanly.

The mistake is then adding too many far secondary stops and turning a focused day into a zigzag.

### Purple Mountain day

For [Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum](/places/sun-yat-sen-mausoleum/) or [Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum](/places/ming-xiaoling-mausoleum/), many travelers still use metro for part of the route, but the day often improves if the final connection or tired return becomes a taxi or Didi.

That is especially true when:

- the weather is hot
- energy is limited
- the same day still wants an evening block afterward

### Qinhuai evening

For an evening around [Confucius Temple and the Qinhuai River](/places/confucius-temple-and-qinhuai-river/) or [Laomendong](/places/laomendong/), walking should do most of the work once you arrive.

This is where Nanjing gets worse if you keep relocating instead of letting one evening district carry the night.

## Common first-time Nanjing transport mistakes

- planning by attraction count instead of by district
- treating metro, airport arrival, and late-night returns as the same kind of problem
- forcing Purple Mountain into an already tight day without protecting the transfer logic
- choosing a hotel that looks central on paper but weakens the evening and morning in practice
- refusing one taxi or Didi when the cheap option has clearly become the worse option
