Many travelers think the hard part of using Didi in China is language. Usually it is not. The hard part is much more basic: downloading the right app, registering with a number that still works in China, setting payment up before the first ride, and understanding how pickups work in busy places like airports and stations.

This guide is written as practical travel advice, using official Beijing foreigner transport guidance checked on **June 17, 2026** plus Didi's current app and download references. Exact app screens can change, but the setup logic stays similar.

## Read this before your first pickup becomes the stressful part of the day

This page is for travelers who want to use Didi as a real trip tool, not just as a vague backup idea.

It is especially useful if:

- you do not speak Chinese
- you expect to arrive late or with luggage
- you want a simpler alternative to figuring out local taxis on the street
- you know some city rides will be much easier by car than by metro

If your bigger question is still when to choose metro and when to pay for a car, keep the broader parent guide [Metro, Taxi, and Ride-Hailing in China: What First-Time Travelers Should Expect](/topics/metro-taxi-ridehailing/) open too.

If you are really trying to judge whether independent travel in China feels practical at all, pair this with [Is China Easy to Travel Independently in 2026?](/topics/is-china-easy-to-travel-independently/).

## The first answer most travelers need

Yes, many foreign travelers can use Didi in China without speaking Chinese. The easiest setup usually looks like this:

- install the China-capable Didi app before the trip
- sign in with a phone number that works during the trip
- add digital payment before the first ride
- use the map pin carefully
- rely on the app's plate number, driver details, and in-app messages rather than on spoken conversation

If those pieces are in place, Didi is often one of the most useful everyday travel apps in China.

## Step 1: Make sure you have the right app

This is the first place some travelers lose time.

DiDi's global sites and app-store listings show multiple Didi app variants, and some app-store pages point users toward a separate China or Greater China version for rides in mainland China. Do not wait until arrival to discover that your installed version is not the one that works for your route.

The safest move is:

- install the app before departure
- open it and confirm it runs normally
- make sure it is the mainland-China-capable version, not only an overseas local market version

## Step 2: Register with a phone number you can still use in China

Beijing's official transport guide for foreigners says both Chinese and foreign phone numbers can work for Didi registration. The important part is not where the number comes from. The important part is whether it is active and reachable during the trip.

That means:

- do not register with a number you cannot receive codes on
- do not assume a home number will be convenient if roaming is disabled
- keep the number available on the same phone you will carry around China

If the number itself becomes unreachable, Didi can become much more annoying than it needs to be.

## Step 3: Set up payment before the first ride

This matters more than many travelers expect.

Beijing's official Didi guide for foreigners lists payment methods such as bank card, WeChat Pay, and Alipay, and explicitly notes that Didi is **not** a cash-friendly system. In other words, do not treat cash as your main fallback.

The calmest setup is:

- link a payment method before departure if possible
- keep Alipay or WeChat Pay working if you rely on them
- do not make your first Didi request while still uncertain whether payment will clear

This is why Didi works best as part of the same planning block as [Cash, Card, Alipay, or WeChat Pay: How to Pay in China](/topics/how-to-pay-in-china/) and the narrower wallet-comparison page [Alipay or WeChat Pay for Tourists in China? What to Set Up First](/topics/alipay-wechat-pay/).

## Step 4: Order the ride carefully

Beijing's official guide says the app accepts both English and Chinese destination input, and that even an area or venue name can be enough. That is good news for travelers.

Still, the real problem is often not the destination. It is the pickup.

When requesting a ride:

- place the pin carefully
- use an obvious pickup point, not a vague side street if possible
- pay attention to which side of the road you are actually standing on
- at airports and stations, follow the designated ride-hailing pickup zones

Many failed Didi moments are not really failed rides. They are failed pickups.

## Step 5: Use the app to communicate instead of improvising

This is where Didi becomes much more foreigner-friendly than some travelers expect.

Beijing's official guide explains that the app includes message tools and quick messages that are automatically translated between English and Chinese. It also notes that most drivers will only speak Chinese.

That means your best communication tools are usually:

- the map pin
- the car plate number
- the live driver location
- in-app quick messages
- the in-app call button only if truly necessary

If the driver cannot find you, short app-based location clarification is usually better than trying to manage the whole conversation out loud.

## Step 6: Confirm the right car before getting in

The app shows the license plate and driver details. Use them.

Beijing's foreigner guide also says the rider may need to verify the trip using the last four digits of the phone number shown in the app. That is a small detail, but a useful one to know before the first ride.

In practice:

- match the plate first
- check the vehicle type
- use the in-app trip details rather than assumptions

## When Didi is especially useful

For many first-time visitors, Didi is most worth using when:

- the weather is bad
- the route has awkward metro transfers
- the city has steep terrain
- you are moving at night
- you have luggage
- energy is low and you do not want a transport puzzle

That is why Didi is often much more important on arrival day and end-of-day returns than in the middle of a smooth sightseeing day.

## What usually goes wrong

### The app version problem

Some travelers discover too late that they installed the wrong regional variant or never checked whether the app actually works for mainland China.

### Pickup confusion

You are on one side of the road, the pin is on the other, the driver is waiting in the official pickup area, and both sides think the other one is wrong.

### Payment not really finished

The app exists, but the wallet is not ready. That is not a Didi problem. That is a setup problem.

### Phone-number trouble

If the number cannot receive verification or is not convenient to use during the trip, the whole experience gets harder.

## Common mistakes

- downloading Didi too late
- not checking whether the app is the right China-ready version
- trying to rely on cash
- setting a vague pickup point in a crowded area
- ignoring airport and station designated ride-hailing pickup zones
- assuming spoken English will solve location confusion faster than the app tools
