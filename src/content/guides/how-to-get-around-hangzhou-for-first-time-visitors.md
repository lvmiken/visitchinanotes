Hangzhou transport usually is not hard because the city lacks options.

It gets fuzzy because first-time visitors often treat very different jobs as if one mode should solve them all equally well:

- airport or station arrival
- crossing the city between hotel zones
- moving around `West Lake`
- reaching `Lingyin Temple` or `Longjing Village`
- getting back comfortably after dinner or a long scenic day

For most trips, the strongest pattern is simpler than it first seems:

- use `metro` for the bigger city moves
- use `walking` for the best parts of West Lake
- use `taxi or Didi` for awkward scenic edges or tired returns
- use `lake buses, shared bikes, or the water bus` only when they clearly improve one part of the day

## The short answer

For many first-time visitors, the strongest Hangzhou transport pattern is:

1. use `metro` for the main cross-city moves
2. use `walking` for the core [West Lake](/places/west-lake/) time
3. use `taxi or Didi` for luggage, rain, late returns, or scenic branches with clumsier last-mile access
4. use `West Lake buses`, `shared bikes`, or the `water bus` only when one specific day clearly benefits from them
5. treat the `hotel area` as part of the transport strategy, not as a separate hotel choice

That usually is enough to make Hangzhou feel calm without overengineering it.

## Why Hangzhou transport gets misread

Hangzhou feels softer than Beijing, Shanghai, or Chongqing.

That can make first-time visitors assume the city is one easy walkable scenic spread.

It is not.

The city often works best when you separate these layers clearly:

- `West Lake` as the scenic core
- `Wulin` or the east-of-lake side as a practical hotel and movement layer
- `Lingyin Temple` as a stronger cultural branch
- `Longjing Village` as a tea-country branch
- `Hefang Street` as a lighter supporting finish rather than a major transport mission

That is why Hangzhou transport feels best when each half day has one clear job.

## When metro is usually the best choice

For many first-time visitors, metro is the strongest answer when:

- the route is a real city-to-city move rather than a scenic shoreline stroll
- the day starts or ends in a practical zone such as `Wulin` or the east-of-lake side
- you are moving between hotel, rail station, and one main sightseeing area
- the weather is fine
- you are not trying to solve every scenic last mile with public transit

Metro is often especially useful for:

- arrival or departure days
- moving between a practical hotel base and the lake zone
- keeping a short stay efficient when Hangzhou is paired with Shanghai
- avoiding extra road traffic on wider city moves

The Hangzhou Xiaoshan Airport official English page currently shows `Metro Lines 1, 7, and 19` serving the airport. The useful takeaway is not that every arrival must use metro. It is that Hangzhou's rail network is a credible first-choice backbone rather than only a local fallback.

## What metro and bus payment usually look like now

Hangzhou's official pages currently describe two especially useful first-time-visitor developments.

The first is that Hangzhou says inbound tourists can use the `Alipay Tourism Code` and related one-code travel tools for transport and other tourism services.

The second is that the city's official Alipay transport note says international visitors can:

- register Alipay with an overseas phone number
- link an international bank card such as `Visa` or `Mastercard`
- tap `Transport`
- use an e-QR code at `bus` and `subway` gates

For many first-time visitors, that means the practical question is no longer "Can I ride public transport?" but "Is public transport still the best answer for this exact leg?"

If payment confidence still is the bigger blocker, keep [Cash, Card, Alipay, or WeChat Pay: How to Pay in China](/topics/how-to-pay-in-china/) open too.

## Taxi or Didi becomes smarter when the city turns scenic instead of efficient

Hangzhou is one of the easier China cities to justify paying more for comfort.

That usually happens when:

- you are carrying luggage from station or airport
- the weather turns hot, humid, or rainy
- you are going to or from `Lingyin Temple`
- you are using `Longjing Village` as a real branch
- the return is late and the final hotel leg feels annoying

That is why many first-time visitors like Hangzhou most when they:

- use metro in the middle of the stay
- use Didi or taxi at the beginning or end of the day

The city gets better when you stop trying to win every transport decision on price alone.

If the app itself still feels like the blocker, go directly to [How to Use Didi in China Without Speaking Chinese](/topics/how-to-use-didi-in-china/).

## Taxi and Didi solve similar problems, even if the booking method is different

For most first-time Hangzhou visitors, the practical difference is simple:

- `Didi` is easier if you want pickup and destination handled in the app
- `street taxi` is still fine for a straightforward ride if one already is there

The Hangzhou airport's official English taxi page also shows that airport taxis use formal pickup areas and meter-based payment, which is a useful signal for first-time arrivals who do not want to improvise after landing.

For many readers, this means:

- `metro` is often the better daytime budget answer
- `taxi or Didi` is often the cleaner answer for luggage or a tired final return

## West Lake is where walking should usually take over

This is the most important Hangzhou transport rule.

Do not plan [West Lake](/places/west-lake/) as if it were only one more metro stop.

The lake usually works best when:

- metro or car gets you to the right side of the area
- walking does the actual scenic work
- one supporting stop such as [Broken Bridge](/places/broken-bridge/) or [Leifeng Pagoda](/places/leifeng-pagoda/) grows naturally out of the lake day

It usually gets worse when:

- you keep hopping around the lake to chase coverage
- you treat every viewpoint like a separate city mission
- you use too much of the day repositioning instead of letting the lake itself carry the time

## West Lake buses are useful selective tools, not the whole plan

Hangzhou's official tourism article on West Lake buses highlights:

- `Loop Buses 51 and 52` circling the lake in opposite directions
- `Route 7` and `Route 87` for broader scenic coverage including the `Lingyin` side

That does not mean you should build the entire stay around bus routes.

It means these buses can help when:

- the weather is hot
- you want to protect leg energy
- you want a more scenic connection between lake highlights
- one part of the day needs support without upgrading all the way to a taxi

For many first-time visitors, they are best used as:

- scenic support on a lake day
- a selective connector toward the Lingyin side
- a comfort tool when walking every segment would weaken the day

## Shared bikes are good for flat scenic stretches, but only selectively

Hangzhou's official pages say foreign residents and visitors can now access the city's iconic red shared bikes through the city's upgraded digital tools.

That is useful because Hangzhou is one of the China cities where bikes can genuinely improve some first-time visitor days.

But they are strongest when:

- the route is flat and scenic
- you already feel comfortable cycling in city traffic conditions
- the day wants one softer lake-side stretch rather than complicated navigation

They are weaker when:

- this is your first day and the city still feels unfamiliar
- the weather is crowded, wet, or very hot
- you are trying to use a bike to rescue a badly planned multi-branch itinerary

For many first-time visitors, shared bikes are a bonus tool, not the default answer.

## The water bus is scenic support, not a core city-movement answer

Hangzhou's official English site highlights `Water Bus Line 7` as a scenic local favorite with upgraded boats.

That matters because Hangzhou can reward one transport choice that also feels like part of the trip.

But for most short first-time stays, the water bus is strongest when:

- the day already wants one softer canal or water-side layer
- you like the idea of a local scenic transition
- the route still stays simple

It is weaker when:

- you are only trying to move efficiently between major first-time priorities
- the stay is very short
- the day already has enough scenic transport experimentation

## Hotel area is the biggest transport decision most travelers make without noticing

This is why [Best Area to Stay in Hangzhou for First-Time Visitors](/guides/hangzhou-where-to-stay/) matters so much.

Use this rough logic:

- choose `east-of-West-Lake` if you want the best first-time scenic balance
- choose `Wulin` if practical movement matters almost as much as scenery
- choose the `Hefang Street / old-core side` only if atmosphere genuinely matters enough to shape the stay

Many Hangzhou transport problems that look like app problems are really hotel-location problems.

The wrong hotel does not only waste time.

It makes a short, calm city feel more scattered than it really is.

## What this looks like on real Hangzhou days

### West Lake day

For a day built around [West Lake](/places/west-lake/), the usual best pattern is:

- metro or car to the right side of the lake zone
- walking for the core scenic block
- one bus, taxi, or selective support move only if it clearly improves the day

This is exactly the kind of day that gets weaker when you overuse transport inside the lake itself.

### Lingyin day

For a day built around [Lingyin Temple](/places/lingyin-temple/), metro may still help with part of the route, but many first-time visitors end up preferring a taxi, Didi, or a selective scenic-bus version for the last stretch.

That is especially true when:

- weather is poor
- energy is limited
- the route also includes a second lighter stop

### Longjing day

For [Longjing Village](/places/longjing-village/), the strongest answer usually is not pure transport efficiency.

It is choosing the mode that keeps the tea-country part of the day feeling soft rather than fragmented.

That often means:

- metro for the larger city leg
- taxi or Didi for the awkward scenic segment
- keeping the whole day lighter overall

### One-night stay

For a short overnight Hangzhou stop, transport usually works best when you stop trying to learn every mode.

What often works:

- metro for the biggest practical move
- walking for the main lake block
- Didi or taxi for one awkward edge

That is usually enough.

## Common first-time Hangzhou transport mistakes

- treating the whole city like one easy walk around the lake
- overusing public transport inside the best scenic parts of West Lake
- forcing Lingyin and Longjing into the same day without protecting the transfers
- choosing a romantic hotel address that weakens the whole stay
- trying to save money on one tired late return that should simply be a taxi or Didi
