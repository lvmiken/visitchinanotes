## How to shape the three days

### Day 1: Arrive, settle in, and let the city open gently

Keep the first day light. Use it to understand the hotel area, ease into local transport, and give the trip a food-led start instead of trying to prove that the city is exciting immediately.

For many first-time visitors, this is the best slot for:

- `Chunxi Road or Taikoo Li` if the trip wants a modern central first look
- one easier evening in [Yulin](/places/yulin/) or near [Jiuyanqiao](/places/jiuyanqiao/) if the group wants food and nightlife more than another formal sight

If the central-first version is already winning, the matching meal page is [Where to Eat Near Chunxi Road and Taikoo Li in Chengdu for First-Time Visitors](/guides/where-to-eat-near-chunxi-road-and-taikoo-li-in-chengdu-for-first-time-visitors/).

This is the best day for the trip's main attraction, especially if panda logistics are part of the plan. Do that first, then leave the rest of the day open enough for food, tea, or a slower neighborhood session.

Use the final day for the version of Chengdu that first-time visitors often remember most: street life, meals, evening rhythm, and one more neighborhood block of time that is not overprogrammed.

- `Wuhou Shrine and Jinli` if the trip wants one classic historical and old-street layer
- `Kuanzhai Alley` if the trip wants one shorter central atmosphere stop rather than a fuller traditional-core block

## What to watch out for

The biggest itinerary mistake is to treat Chengdu as if every day must revolve around one hard-ticket landmark. The city usually becomes more enjoyable when you let mood and meals carry part of the structure.

## Common mistakes

- planning Chengdu with the same speed as Beijing or Shanghai
- stacking the panda visit and too many other fixed commitments into one day
- giving the city no room for evening food or slower wandering
