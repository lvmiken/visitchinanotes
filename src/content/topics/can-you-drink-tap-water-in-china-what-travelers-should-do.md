One of the most common first-trip questions about China is also one of the most ordinary:

`Can I drink the tap water?`

The useful answer is not dramatic.

It is simply this:

- do not rely on untreated tap water for everyday drinking
- use boiled, bottled, or otherwise clearly safe drinking water instead

That one rule solves most of the anxiety.

## Source check

This page was checked against the World Health Organization's current [Drinking-water](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/drinking-water) guidance on **June 27, 2026**. WHO's current guidance emphasizes that microbiologically contaminated drinking water remains a major health risk globally and that safe drinking-water depends on water being available when needed and free from contamination. I am applying that public-health logic conservatively for foreign travelers: when you do not fully know the local building, plumbing, or treatment situation, the sensible travel default is to avoid untreated tap water and use safer drinking options.

## The short answer

For most foreign visitors, the safest simple routine is:

- do not drink untreated tap water directly
- use sealed bottled water, properly boiled water, or other clearly safe drinking-water options
- stay relaxed about normal hot tea and cooked food unless you have a very specific health concern

## 1. The best travel rule is simple, not technical

You do not need to become a water engineer to travel well in China.

You just need one clean default.

For most travelers, that default is:

- direct tap water for drinking: `no`
- boiled or clearly safe drinking water: `yes`

## 2. Hotels usually make this easier than travelers fear

Many travelers imagine this question turning into a daily headache.

In reality, many hotels make it fairly manageable through some combination of:

- bottled water
- in-room kettles
- easy convenience-store access nearby

The point is not to assume every hotel handles it the same way.

The point is that you usually can set a low-friction routine quickly.

## 3. Tea, coffee, and restaurant meals are not the same question as drinking from the tap

This is where travelers often overcorrect.

The main issue is not every sip of liquid in the country.

It is untreated everyday drinking water.

That is why many visitors stay perfectly calm about:

- hot tea
- coffee made through a normal café setup
- soups and cooked meals

while still avoiding plain untreated tap water for routine drinking.

## 4. If you are sensitive, lower the friction further

Some travelers are simply more cautious, more sensitive, or traveling with children.

For them, the easiest low-stress version is:

- keep sealed bottled water in the room
- use bottled or cooled boiled water for drinking
- use the same safer water for teeth brushing if that helps you stay calm

That is not overreacting.

It is just choosing a simple routine.

## 5. The bigger goal is to remove one unnecessary daily anxiety

This topic matters because tiny uncertainties can drain a trip.

If water feels unclear, travelers start second-guessing:

- brushing teeth
- taking medicine
- filling a reusable bottle
- waking up thirsty at night

If that uncertainty matters because you are traveling with diabetes or another routine that depends on steadier hydration and meal timing, the more specific prep page is [Traveling in China With Diabetes: What to Prepare Before You Go](/topics/traveling-in-china-with-diabetes-what-to-prepare/).

One good routine removes all of that.

## 6. What most first-time visitors actually need

They do not need a giant health lecture.

They need to know that the reasonable, non-panicked travel version is:

- do not drink untreated tap water directly
- use safer drinking water options that are easy to confirm
- move on with the trip

## Common mistakes

- assuming all tap water is automatically fine for direct drinking because the hotel looks upscale
- swinging to the opposite extreme and panicking about every tea or restaurant drink
- carrying no hydration plan at all for long sightseeing days
- turning a simple daily routine into a constant research project
