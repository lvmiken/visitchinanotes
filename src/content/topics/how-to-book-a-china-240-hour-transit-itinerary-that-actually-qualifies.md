Many travelers think the hard part of the 240-hour transit policy is nationality.

In practice, the harder part is often ticket structure.

This page was checked against current official Shanghai international-services pages including the main [Visa-free transit explainer](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-VisaFreeTransit/index.html), the current FAQ example [Does my London-Shanghai-Hong Kong journey qualify for the 240-hr visa-free transit?](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Visa2/20251020/b6d40e72778f4ea1a83a20f4664341d4.html), and the current FAQ page [Who is eligible for the 240-hour visa-free transit policy in China?](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-FAQs-TravelinShanghai/20250123/06f41af45c36475e903d864856e90702.html), all checked on **June 28, 2026**.

## The short answer

A qualifying 240-hour transit itinerary usually needs all of these:

- an eligible passport
- a designated entry port
- a confirmed onward ticket to a **third country or region**
- the whole stop staying within the rules of the entry region

The most common booking mistake is not the passport.

It is building the wrong flight pattern.

## What a qualifying itinerary is really trying to prove

You are not proving only that you want to visit China.

You are proving that you are:

- entering China in transit
- continuing onward within the allowed time
- and doing so through a designated port and region

That is why `ticket structure` matters so much.

## The cleanest pattern

The cleanest 240-hour pattern usually looks like:

`Country or region A → China → Country or region B`

That is the shape most travelers should aim for.

## A useful official Hong Kong example

Current official Shanghai FAQ material explicitly says that:

`London → Shanghai → Hong Kong`

qualifies for the 240-hour transit policy.

That example matters because it helps many travelers understand that:

- Hong Kong can count as the onward region
- and a qualifying stopover does not have to continue only to a totally unrelated long-haul country

## What to confirm before you book

### 1. The passport

Make sure you are from a currently eligible country.

If that is still unclear, start first with [China's 240-Hour Visa-Free Transit, Clearly Explained: Eligibility, Routing, and Common Mistakes](/topics/china-240-hour-visa-free-transit/).

### 2. The entry region

Do not book by vague city name alone.

Make sure the arrival airport or port is part of the current designated framework for the region you want to use.

If that still is the main blocker, go next to [China's 240-Hour Transit Policy: Which Airports and Cities Actually Qualify?](/topics/chinas-240-hour-transit-policy-which-airports-and-cities-actually-qualify/).

### 3. The onward ticket

Current official Shanghai material says travelers need:

- a connecting ticket
- to a third country or region
- with a confirmed seat and date
- within 240 hours

That is the heart of the booking logic.

### 4. The route shape

Even when the tickets exist, the route still should look coherent enough that airline staff and border officers can understand it quickly.

## What kind of ticketing usually feels safest

For many travelers, the safest stopover booking is:

- one obvious arrival into China
- one obvious onward flight or ticket
- one region only
- one stopover city whose pacing actually fits the trip

This is not the moment for a heroic multi-city chain.

## What often makes the booking risky

The route becomes riskier when:

- the onward segment is still fuzzy
- the traveler is guessing whether the arrival airport qualifies
- the ticket technically exists but the regional movement plan is unrealistic
- the itinerary needs too much explanation at check-in

## Do not build the sightseeing before the ticket logic

A common mistake is to plan:

- museums
- hotels
- restaurant nights
- and day trips

before confirming whether the transit frame itself works.

The better order is:

1. qualify the entry rule
2. qualify the ticket pattern
3. choose the stopover region
4. only then shape the city stay

If you notice that your "stopover" idea already depends on normal domestic-style route logic inside China, step out of transit mode before you keep booking around the wrong frame. For example, `Beijing + Xi'an`, `Beijing + Shanghai`, `Xi'an + Chengdu`, or `Shanghai + Suzhou + Hangzhou` are usually better solved as normal route pages rather than as transit add-ons that you hope will somehow still fit.

## The strongest first-time stopover instinct

If this is your first time trying to use the policy, choose the stopover that is:

- easiest to explain
- easiest to arrive into
- easiest to use inside the allowed region

That is often why `Shanghai` beats a more romantic but harder stopover idea.

## Common mistakes

- booking the stopover city before confirming the onward pattern
- assuming any exit from China counts the same way
- forgetting that Hong Kong can help some routes but does not solve every route automatically
- trying to squeeze two or three different China moods into one transit permit
