This question still scares travelers because old and new Hainan entry rules keep getting mixed together in search results.

The short answer is still:

- for most travelers using the classic `59-country` Hainan regional visa-free policy, a travel agency is **not** the default requirement
- some separate Hainan-related group arrangements do still exist
- those are exactly what people keep confusing with ordinary independent entry

## What I rechecked for this page

I rechecked this page on **July 11, 2026** against the National Immigration Administration's [Regional Visa-Free Entry Policies for Foreign Nationals](https://en.nia.gov.cn/n147418/n147463/c180637/content.html), the NIA explainer [Port Visa and Regional Visa-Free Entry Policies](https://en.nia.gov.cn/n147418/n147463/c156078/content.html), the NIA notice [Foreign Tourist Groups from Hong Kong and Macao SARs Can Enter and Stay for 144 Hours in Hainan Without Visas](https://en.nia.gov.cn/n147413/c166593/content.html), and Hainan's official [Ports open to visa-free policy](https://en.hainan.gov.cn/englishsite/mr7/202506/d9b1aa47bb9b4f3c8f12503850e06357.shtml?ddtab=true). The wording below is an editorial reading of how those official pages separate independent regional entry from group-based entry frameworks.

## The editorial answer

If your passport fits the classic Hainan regional policy and you are flying directly into Hainan for a normal island stay, the safe planning assumption is:

`You usually do not need to build the trip around a travel agency.`

That is the practical answer most independent travelers are looking for.

## Why the internet still makes this look messier than it is

There are two different memories floating around in search:

- the classic Hainan regional visa-free framework
- separate Hainan-related group-entry arrangements, especially through `Hong Kong` or `Macau`

When those get flattened into one headline, travelers start assuming every Hainan-friendly entry path still requires a tour-company structure.

That is the confusion to avoid.

## What the official pages actually tell you

The NIA regional-policy page still describes the classic Hainan framework as:

- ordinary passport holders from `59 countries`
- up to `30 days`
- entry through Hainan's authorized ports
- stay within `Hainan Province`

The older NIA policy explainer is also useful because it explicitly says those eligible travelers may enter in tourist groups, at the invitation of inviting entities, **or independently**.

That last part matters.

It is the clearest official signal that ordinary independent entry is part of the classic framework.

## Where the travel-agency idea is still real

The group logic did not come from nowhere.

The NIA also maintains a separate policy for some foreign tourist groups entering Hainan from `Hong Kong` or `Macau`, and that framework is group-based by design.

So the correct conclusion is not:

`Travel-agency advice is always wrong.`

It is:

`Travel-agency advice may describe a different Hainan entry framework than the one I am actually using.`

## So can you just book a flight to Sanya or Haikou yourself?

For many travelers under the classic framework, yes in principle.

The right sequence is:

1. confirm your passport fits the policy you think it fits
2. confirm you are entering through an authorized Hainan port
3. confirm the trip is being treated as a Hainan stay, not as an assumed mainland-through-route
4. then book like an independent traveler

That is much safer than reverse-engineering old group-tour advice.

## When to slow down and double-check

Be more careful if:

- your passport qualification is not obvious
- you are mixing Free Trade Port messaging with the classic regional policy
- the route continues to mainland cities after Hainan
- your entry pattern depends on `Hong Kong` or `Macau` group logic

Those are the cases where the wrong shortcut can still break the route.

## The editorial default

For most first-time visitors using Hainan's classic regional visa-free entry:

- think `independent island trip`
- not `tour-agency filing exercise`

That is the assumption most aligned with the current official policy structure.

## Common mistakes

- reading one outdated blog and assuming every Hainan entry still needs a travel agency
- mixing together the classic `59-country` rule and the separate `Hong Kong` or `Macau` tourist-group rule
- solving the Hainan entry question but forgetting that mainland continuation is a separate question
