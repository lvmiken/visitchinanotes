When travelers ask whether `foreign cards work at China night markets`, they usually mean something more specific:

`Can I just tap or swipe my normal Visa or Mastercard at a food stall and keep walking?`

For many first-time visitors, that is the wrong expectation.

This page was checked against current official payment guidance on **July 7, 2026**, including the State Council and People's Bank of China's official [Guide to Payment Services in China](https://english.www.gov.cn/news/202403/15/content_WS65f3bcb1c6d0868f4e8e51b5.html), the State Council's broader [visit-China guidelines page](https://english.www.gov.cn/services/visitchina/641d29fec6d0f528699dc1df), and the current payment-cluster pages already live on this site. Those official sources are enough to confirm the broader reality: China has improved payment access for overseas visitors, but first-time travelers still should not treat small-market merchants like hotel desks or airport counters.

## The short answer

For many first-time visitors:

- do **not** expect foreign physical cards to be the main direct payment method at street stalls
- do expect `Alipay` or `WeChat Pay` linked to an international card to matter much more
- do carry some cash for backup

The practical answer is not:

`My card works somewhere in China, so it will work at every market stall.`

The practical answer is:

`Small-merchant payment needs its own plan.`

## Why foreign cards are the wrong default assumption

Night markets and street stalls are not the same as:

- hotels
- airports
- major malls
- chain restaurants

Small snack vendors and market stalls often operate through:

- QR-code payments
- mobile wallets
- fast low-ticket transactions

That means the trip should not rely on the stall having:

- a card terminal
- an international-card flow
- or the patience to troubleshoot one tiny purchase

## What usually works better

For most first-time visitors, the best small-purchase setup is:

- one working mobile wallet
- one backup mobile wallet or physical card
- some cash

## When a foreign physical card might still work

Sometimes it can still work:

- at larger fixed merchants inside a market district
- at more formal restaurants near a market
- at shops that operate more like standard retail than temporary stalls

But that is not the same thing as saying:

- every snack stall accepts it
- every QR merchant has a card terminal
- or your first-night dumpling stop will pause the line for a card issue

The right mental model is:

`possible in some places, unsafe as the main plan for stalls`

## Why Alipay and WeChat Pay matter more here

The official national payment guide makes clear that foreign visitors now have more usable payment options in China than before.

But the small-merchant reality still points in one direction:

mobile wallets solve more everyday China-travel problems than foreign physical cards do.

That is especially true for:

- night markets
- snack streets
- dessert stalls
- convenience-style tiny purchases

If the broader phone stack still is not ready, keep [What Apps You Need for a China Trip](/topics/what-apps-you-need-for-a-china-trip/) nearby too.

## Should you still carry cash?

Yes.

Not because cash should become your full China payment plan.

Because cash is still useful when:

- the wallet app asks for one more verification step
- the signal is bad
- the first few small purchases feel awkward
- one very small vendor keeps things simpler with cash

The mistake is not carrying cash.

The mistake is carrying `only` cash or `only` one app.

## What this means in real travel situations

If you are going to:

- a Chongqing snack street
- a Guangzhou food district
- a Qingdao beer-and-seafood evening
- a Chengdu late-night lane

the safest expectation is:

- mobile wallet first
- cash backup
- physical foreign card mainly for bigger merchants elsewhere

That is usually enough to remove the anxiety.

## Common mistakes

- assuming "China accepts cards now" means every stall will take your foreign physical card
- bringing no cash because one wallet seems linked
- setting up payment only after landing and then testing it at a crowded stall
- confusing a fixed shop in a market district with a real street-food stall
- treating small-vendor payment as the same problem as hotel or train payment
