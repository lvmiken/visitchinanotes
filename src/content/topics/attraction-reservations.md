The biggest reservation mistake is not booking too little. It is giving every famous sight the same planning weight.

That is how travelers end up protecting the wrong things, locking too much too early, and turning a flexible city day into a brittle schedule.

This page is here to separate the bookings that actually shape the route from the ones that only look important because they are famous.

## Who this page is for

This page is for travelers who already have a mostly stable route and now need to decide which tickets or timed entries deserve real protection.

It is especially useful if you are asking:

- what truly needs to be reserved in advance?
- what can stay flexible?
- which sight will damage the day the most if it fails?
- how much of the city should be arranged around one booking?

If your real problem already is one specific attraction, the narrower attraction page usually is better than this parent hub.

## The editorial answer

Do not try to prebook the whole trip.

Instead, identify the one or two reservations per city that actually control:

- whether the city day works
- when the day must start
- whether the route around it still flows
- whether the city stop remains worth doing on your dates

Everything else should compete for flexibility, not for equal urgency.

## Start with three questions

Before you reserve anything, ask:

1. Which sight is the real reason this city is in the trip?
2. Which failed booking would hurt the day the most?
3. Which parts of the day can safely stay unprotected?

That filter is usually more useful than any giant list of "book this / do not book this."

## What usually deserves early attention

These are the booking types that most often deserve real protection:

- signature attractions that define the city stop
- timed-entry visits that force the whole day to organize around them
- excursions far from the hotel core
- high-demand places on tight city stays
- sights whose value drops sharply if you miss the right slot or date

If a place is one of the main reasons the city made the route, it should be treated like an anchor, not like an optional same-day decision.

## What usually can stay looser

Many travelers create unnecessary rigidity by trying to reserve everything.

Often, these can stay more flexible unless peak demand clearly changes the equation:

- lower-priority museums
- neighborhoods and strolling areas
- ordinary viewpoints
- secondary food stops
- anything that would be nice but would not damage the city if it moved

The goal is not zero planning. The goal is protecting only what truly deserves protection.

## How to tell whether something is a real anchor

A reservation is probably an anchor if:

- the city feels incomplete without it
- the timing of the day depends on it
- it affects trains, flights, or long transfers
- you have limited date flexibility
- missing it would force you to rethink the city stop, not just feel mildly disappointed

If missing it would be annoying but not structurally damaging, it probably is not your first reservation priority.

## Where reservations start changing the wider route

This is the point where ticket logic stops being a small detail and starts influencing transport and pacing.

In Beijing, a strong timed entry can change whether the whole central day remains realistic.

In Xi'an, a major excursion booking can determine whether the city works as a short stop at all.

In Shanghai, a full-day anchor can stop being "just a ticket" and become the organizing principle of the day.

## A good booking order for most trips

This sequence usually works better than protecting everything at once:

1. lock the city dates
2. identify the one or two true anchors
3. reserve those first
4. place transport and hotel logic around them
5. let the rest of the day stay lighter

That order is safer because it keeps the important booking in charge without letting the whole city become over-scripted.

## Common mistakes

- trying to reserve everything instead of protecting the true anchor
- giving a secondary attraction the same urgency as a city-defining one
- leaving the most important timed visit until after arrival
- building a day that depends on multiple strict bookings
- forgetting that one major reservation often deserves a lighter surrounding day
