This is the question that turns Hainan from an easy island chapter into a route risk.

## The editorial answer

If you are relying on Hainan's classic regional visa-free entry policy, the safe answer is usually:

**No, not onward into mainland China under that same Hainan-only framework.**

If the route includes `Shanghai`, `Beijing`, `Guangzhou`, or another mainland stop after Hainan, then the mainland part usually needs to be supported by some separate China entry logic.

That is the practical answer most travelers need.

## What I rechecked for this page

This page was rechecked on **July 11, 2026** against the National Immigration Administration's [Regional Visa-Free Entry Policies for Foreign Nationals](https://en.nia.gov.cn/n147418/n147463/c180637/content.html) and Hainan's official [Ports open to visa-free policy](https://en.hainan.gov.cn/englishsite/mr7/202506/d9b1aa47bb9b4f3c8f12503850e06357.shtml?ddtab=true). The conclusion here is a planning inference from those official descriptions: when the entry framework is the classic Hainan regional policy, the permitted stay is framed around entering through authorized Hainan ports and staying within Hainan Province.

## Why this question confuses so many people

Travelers often use the phrase `visa-free Hainan` to answer three different questions at once:

- can I enter `Hainan` without a visa?
- can I then continue to the rest of mainland China?
- can I leave and re-enter later under the same logic?

Those are not the same question.

The fact that the first one may be yes does not make the second one yes.

## What the classic Hainan policy actually does

The current official NIA wording still describes the classic Hainan framework in narrow terms:

- ordinary passport holders from `59 countries`
- up to `30 days`
- entry through authorized Hainan ports
- stay limited within `Hainan Province`

That last point is the one that matters most here.

If the stay is limited within Hainan Province, then `Sanya -> Shanghai` is not something you should assume is covered by that same regional entry permission.

## So can you continue to Beijing or Shanghai?

Under the classic Hainan regional policy alone, usually no as a planning assumption.

That means routes like these should not be booked casually:

- `Sanya -> Shanghai`
- `Haikou -> Beijing`
- `Wanning -> Guangzhou`

unless you have already confirmed what separate rule carries the mainland segment.

## When the answer changes

The answer changes only when the mainland part of the route is being supported by something else.

That may include:

- your passport separately qualifying for China's broader ordinary visa-free entry
- another distinct China entry framework
- a transit-based structure that genuinely fits the route

The important point is that the mainland permission would come from that **other** framework, not from stretching Hainan's regional one beyond what it says.

## The practical route rule

If the route includes both:

- a Hainan stay
- and one or more mainland cities afterward

then the right planning question is not:

`Can I get into Hainan?`

It is:

`What policy carries the mainland part of this trip?`

That is the question that prevents broken bookings.

## A safe default if you are still unsure

If you have not separately confirmed broader China entry rights, treat Hainan's classic regional visa-free entry as:

- good for entering Hainan
- good for a Hainan-only chapter
- not the rule you should rely on for mainland continuation

That default is strict, but it is the right kind of strict.

## The common mistake

The usual mistake is sequencing the logic backward:

1. book Hainan because `visa-free` sounds easy
2. add `Shanghai` or `Beijing` later
3. only afterward ask what actually covers the mainland leg

That is not a policy nuance problem. It is a route-order problem.
