Beijing feels much easier when the plan respects geography.

## Day 1: Central imperial landmarks

Use the first full day for the core ceremonial center of the city. This is the day to group the most obvious first-visit priorities together instead of mixing them with farther districts.

## Day 2: One dedicated Great Wall day

Keep the Wall as its own day. Trying to combine it with too much else often turns the best day of the trip into the most tiring one. Return with enough evening flexibility rather than planning a second dense sightseeing block.

## Day 3: Temples, hutong atmosphere, and slower movement

This day works best when it shifts the pace. Use it for neighborhood texture, temple time, and a calmer view of the city that is not just about headline landmarks.

## Day 4: Museum or park priority, then departure buffer

The final day should stay lighter and more adaptable. This is the right place for one more major priority, a park-based morning, or a cleaner departure setup if train or flight timing matters later.

## Do not build every day around cross-city transfers

The route usually breaks down when too many distant points are mixed together. Beijing rewards travelers who cluster priorities by area and leave recovery space between the heaviest days.
