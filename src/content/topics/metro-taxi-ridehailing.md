City transport in China is often very manageable, but only when the route and hotel choice support it.

This page is the broad city-transport hub. It works best before you choose hotel areas or assume that every sightseeing day will move as easily as it looks on a map.

## Who this page is for

This page is for travelers who are not asking, "Does China have metro?" They are asking:

- when should I just take the metro?
- when is a taxi or ride-hailing worth the money?
- how much should hotel location reduce daily transport pain?
- why does a route that looks short on a map still feel tiring?

If you already know your real problem is ride-hailing, go straight to [How to Use Didi in China Without Speaking Chinese](/topics/how-to-use-didi-in-china/).

If the bigger question is your whole phone toolkit for getting around, keep [What Apps You Need for a China Trip](/topics/what-apps-you-need-for-a-china-trip/) open too.

## The short answer

For many first-time visitors:

- **metro** is best when the route is direct, energy is normal, and the hotel base is already good
- **taxi or ride-hailing** is worth it when luggage, weather, fatigue, or awkward transfers would make metro feel worse than the savings are worth
- the **hotel area** often matters more than choosing the perfect transport mode each time

The common mistake is trying to optimize every ride separately instead of building days that need fewer painful rides in the first place.

## Start with these three questions

Before you choose a transport mode, ask:

1. Is the route direct enough that metro still feels easy?
2. Would luggage, weather, stairs, or fatigue make the cheap option a bad trade?
3. Is the real problem actually the hotel location rather than the ride itself?

Those questions usually get you closer to the right answer than comparing metro versus car in the abstract.

## When metro is usually the better choice

Metro is often the strongest option when:

- the route is simple and direct
- you are moving at a normal daytime hour
- you do not have heavy luggage
- the city is flat enough or the walking sections are manageable
- you want predictable cost and timing

For many travelers, metro feels excellent once the day has already started well.

## When Didi or taxi is the smarter travel decision

For many first-time visitors, the car option is strongest when the day includes:

- airport or station arrival
- awkward last-mile hotel logic
- steep terrain or too many transfers
- late return with low energy
- shopping bags, children, or heavier luggage

## When taxi or ride-hailing is usually the better choice

Pay more for the easier ride when:

- the weather is hot, cold, or wet
- the route includes too many transfers
- the day is ending and your energy is low
- you are carrying shopping, luggage, or tired children
- the city has terrain or vertical movement that punishes "cheap but awkward" decisions

Some days are easiest on metro. Others are better by car, especially when:

- the weather is draining
- the route is spread out
- the city has more vertical movement than expected

That is not laziness. It is often just good trip management.

If the ride-hailing part itself is the blocker, go directly to [How to Use Didi in China Without Speaking Chinese](/topics/how-to-use-didi-in-china/) instead of staying on this broader page.

## City style matters more than many travelers expect

Not all Chinese cities ask the same thing from your transport plan.

### Cities where metro often carries the day

Places like Shanghai usually reward travelers who choose a smart hotel area and use metro for the majority of normal sightseeing days.

### Cities where terrain or route shape changes the answer

Places like Chongqing can make "short distance" feel much heavier than the map suggests. A ride that seems optional on paper can be the choice that saves the day in reality.

### Cities where mixed strategy is strongest

For many trips, the best answer is metro during the core day and taxi or ride-hailing for the awkward first or last stretch.

## Hotel choice is the real transport multiplier

Many travelers try to solve transport with apps when the better solution was choosing a better base.

If the hotel area is right:

- the first ride of the day is easier
- the midday reset is easier
- the evening return is easier
- fewer moments feel like logistics management

If the hotel area is wrong, even a good metro system can feel like work.

## What to watch out for

### Map distance can be misleading

A route that looks reasonable on the map can still involve stairs, long interchange walking, poor weather exposure, or a bad end-of-day return.

### The cheapest option is not always the smartest one

Saving a small amount can be a bad trade if it costs energy you need for the rest of the day.

### Arrival-day transport should be judged differently

What works on day three may not be the right answer on day one with luggage and low energy.

## Common city pattern shortcuts

### Shanghai-style days

Metro often carries the day well if the hotel base is good and the route stays district-based.

### Beijing-style days

Metro is strong, but city scale still means district planning matters more than readers expect.

### Chongqing-style days

Distance, terrain, and vertical movement can make ride-hailing much more valuable than the map suggests.

## Common mistakes

- choosing a hotel without thinking about the daily return route
- assuming metro is automatically best because it is cheaper
- overusing taxi because the hotel base is poorly chosen
- ignoring how weather and fatigue change transport decisions
- planning every day as if the city moves as easily as the map suggests

The best local transport strategy is usually the one that keeps the day feeling smooth enough to enjoy the city, not the one that saves a few minutes on paper.
