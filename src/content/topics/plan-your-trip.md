Many first-time visitors make the same planning mistake: they start by collecting too many landmarks before settling the logistics that shape the whole trip.

## Who this page is for

This page is for travelers who already know they want to visit China, but have not yet turned that idea into a workable first route.

It is especially useful if you are still deciding:

- how many cities actually fit
- whether the trip should be fast or comfortable
- how much intercity movement is realistic
- what should be solved before you start dropping attractions into each day

## The short answer

For most first-time visitors, the best China trip is lighter than the first draft.

That usually means:

- fewer cities
- fewer hotel changes
- more respect for transfer days
- practical basics solved early

The strongest routes feel clear before they feel full.

## Build around constraints first

Before choosing landmarks, lock the decisions that truly control the route:

- total trip length
- number of cities
- intercity transport style
- season
- arrival city
- payment and internet readiness

Start by deciding:

- which cities fit their trip length
- what transport assumptions are realistic
- how dense each day should be

## How many cities usually fit?

### Under 7 days

One main city plus one easy add-on is often enough.

### Around 8 to 10 days

Two anchor cities is usually the sweet spot for many first-time trips.

### Around 12 to 14 days

Three cities can work well, but only if transfer days are treated as real travel and not hidden in wishful planning.

The mistake is not moving around. The mistake is pretending moves are free.

## Let transfers count as real travel time

Moving between cities is not only about the train or flight itself. It also means packing, checking out, reaching the station or airport, and settling into the next place. A route that looks efficient on paper can still feel rushed in practice.

Every extra move should earn its place by doing at least one of these:

- adding a clearly different experience
- improving the route logic
- reducing backtracking
- making the overall trip more enjoyable, not just more impressive

## What to solve before you fill the days

Many trips get harder because the order is wrong.

Before building detailed sightseeing days, solve:

- where the trip should start
- which season suits the route
- how payments and internet will work
- whether any anchor attractions need reservations
- whether train or flight decisions will shape the city order

That is why pages like [Where to Start Planning a First Trip to China](/topics/where-to-start/), [Best Time to Visit China for a First Trip](/topics/best-time-to-visit-china/), and [How Foreign Tourists Can Use Alipay and WeChat Pay in China](/topics/alipay-wechat-pay/) are route-building pages, not side details.

## What a strong first-trip structure often looks like

For many readers, a workable route follows this sequence:

1. choose the right anchor city
2. decide whether the trip can support one, two, or three cities
3. choose season and broad transport logic
4. solve arrival, internet, and payments
5. choose hotel areas
6. only then shape day-by-day sightseeing

That order prevents a lot of avoidable rework.

## Common mistakes

- choosing too many cities before checking total days
- ignoring how tiring transfer days become in practice
- building sightseeing days before solving hotel, payment, or arrival basics
- treating rail or flight time as the whole move
- adding hotel changes that do not clearly improve the trip

## Reduce uncertainty before you add more sights

The strongest plans usually come from answering a few practical questions clearly before adding more attractions.
